# Source and claim evaluation

## Build a claim ledger

For each material claim record:

- exact claim and required precision;
- source URL and final URL after redirects;
- author, publisher, publication/update/access dates, version, jurisdiction, and scope;
- primary evidence location such as section, table, page, dataset field, commit, or filing;
- whether the wording is quoted, paraphrased, calculated, or inferred;
- corroborating or conflicting source;
- confidence, caveat, and unresolved gap.

Do not attach one citation to a paragraph containing several claims unless the source supports each one.

## Evaluate proximity and incentives

Ask:

- Who directly observed, measured, enacted, published, or maintains the fact?
- Is this the source of record, an interpretation, a reseller, an affiliate, or a copy?
- What commercial, political, institutional, legal, or reputational incentive affects framing?
- Is methodology visible: sample, exclusions, definitions, units, uncertainty, funding, preregistration, and corrections?
- Does the source distinguish publication date, event date, effective date, and latest update?
- Is the source authoritative for this jurisdiction, version, plan, platform, or population?

Primary does not mean unbiased, and independent does not mean expert. Use both where incentives or interpretation matter.

## Check provenance without overclaiming

- Follow citations to the original paper, dataset, filing, standard, release artifact, transcript, or official record.
- Verify DOI or other persistent identifier metadata against the publisher and registration agency.
- Check corrections, expressions of concern, retractions, errata, superseding standards, amended laws, and replaced releases.
- Use repository commit, tag, signature, checksum, provenance, or release notes when actual implementation matters.
- Treat C2PA credentials and similar provenance as evidence about origin and edits, not proof that content is true.
- Treat absence of provenance metadata as unknown, not automatic evidence of fabrication.

## Resolve conflicts

Classify the disagreement before choosing a source:

- different dates or versions;
- different jurisdiction, population, plan, platform, or definitions;
- measurement, unit, denominator, or sampling differences;
- preliminary versus final data;
- official status versus practical interpretation;
- correction, retraction, or supersession;
- genuine unresolved conflict.

Prefer the source of record for status and the strongest transparent methodology for empirical effect. Cite both when each governs a different part. Narrow the conclusion when the conflict remains.

## Calibrate language

- Use `shows` only for evidence that directly supports the result.
- Use `reports`, `states`, or `claims` for attributed assertions.
- Use `suggests` for limited or indirect evidence.
- Label calculations and inferences explicitly.
- Say `I did not find` only after a search proportionate to the claim; do not convert it to `there is none` without a complete source of record.

Keep uncertainty tied to the specific claim rather than adding a generic disclaimer to an otherwise overconfident answer.
